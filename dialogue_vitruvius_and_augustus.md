# **Dialogue of Vitruvius and Augustus**

**Characters:**

* **Vitruvius** — An aging architect, his skin mapped with the dust of marble and limestone. He carries the heavy scroll of his *De architectura*, his mind obsessed with the "perfect order" of proportion.
* **Augustus** — The Emperor of Rome, draped in purple that cannot hide the frailty of his frame. He looks upon the city as a map of his own will, yet feels the encroaching decay of his legacy.

**Setting:**
*August 19, 14 AD — Nola. A terrace overlooking a crumbling villa garden as the sun dips below the horizon. The air is stagnant, heavy with the scent of dried oleander and the metallic tang of an approaching storm. A single, fallen Corinthian column lies in the grass—the “Silent Third Presence”—half-consumed by creeping ivy, its acanthus leaves long ago smoothed away by rain and neglect.*

---

## **I. The Arrival of the Design**

**Vitruvius:**
(Tracing the air with a trembling finger, visualizing a phantom structure)
Caesar, do you see it? The symmetry is absolute. By aligning the temple’s breadth to the span of the human form, we do not merely build; we mirror the divine mechanics of the cosmos. Every arch, every ratio, is a hymn to the order that governs the stars. If we arrange the world according to these eternal measures, we bind the chaos of existence into a fortress of reason. The Empire is the architecture of the spirit made manifest. Is it not a miracle that we, fragile as we are, can anchor the heavens to the earth?

**Augustus:**
(Rubbing his tired eyes, he glances at the horizon)
I see only the shadows lengthening, Vitruvius. You speak of anchoring the heavens, but I look at the stone and see only the weight that pulls it down. My life has been a long struggle to impose order upon the chaos of the provinces, yet I feel it slipping. The "order" you preach—is it a shield against the dark, or merely a way to measure how quickly we are being forgotten? Even now, the marble we quarried at such cost yearns to return to the dust of the quarry.

---

## **II. The Burden of the Stone**

**Vitruvius:**
It is not merely stone; it is *ratio*—the soul’s protest against the formless void. When I designed the forums, I sought to create a vessel for human greatness, a space where virtue is given a shape. To build is to participate in the act of creation itself. We take the *natura* that is raw and wild, and we refine it until it reflects our own divine capacity for logic.

**Augustus:**
(Watching a spider crawl over the cracked base of the fallen column)
You speak of "refinement" as if the earth cares for your columns. I have conquered the world, yet I am haunted by the *nostalgia* for a stability that never truly existed. I built for eternity, but I see only the ruin-to-be. You treat the column as a triumph of mathematics; I see the "ruin" as its natural conclusion. Every monument I raised was an attempt to bargain with time, but time does not negotiate with the architect. It only waits for the ivy to climb, for the frost to crack the joints, and for the silence to reclaim the stone.

---

## **III. The Arid Truth**

**Augustus:**
Listen, old man. It is *la natura matrigna*. She grants us the stone so that we may amuse ourselves by carving it, only to break it when we are finished. She feeds us the illusion that our cities are eternal, while underneath, the tectonic plates grind with the indifference of a machine. We share *l’infelicità comune*: the builder, the king, and the spider all labor for a house that will not stand.

The "Arid Truth" is that your *De architectura* is a song sung in a vacuum. The universe does not possess a center, and it certainly does not possess a plan. There is only *il nulla*—the vast, uncalculated space between our desires and the cold reality of the ground. The *gelo* of this truth is that your geometry is just a thin veil drawn over an abyss. Order is a human hallucination; chaos is the only constant.

**Vitruvius:**
(His voice faltering, his hands clutching the scroll to his chest)
Then… the years spent calculating the perfect curve? The devotion to the golden mean? Was it all a hollow gesture? If the ruin is the destiny of the monument, then every act of creation is just a slower way of witnessing our own collapse. Is there no permanence in beauty?

**Augustus:**
(He looks at the fallen column, now almost entirely hidden by the ivy)
I once thought the same. I thought the throne was a pivot upon which the fate of all humanity turned. But as the sun sets, I realize the throne is just a seat, and the empire is just a footprint in the mud that the next rain will erase. I mourn the dream of eternity—it was the most beautiful architecture I ever inhabited. But nostalgia is only a trap, Vitruvius. It blinds us to the truth that we were never the masters of the stone; we were merely its temporary servants.

---

## **IV. The Final Irony**

**Vitruvius:**
(He lets the scroll slip from his lap; it unrolls slightly, revealing diagrams of perfect circles in the dirt)
I was so certain that if I could capture the divine proportion, the world would have to acknowledge us. But it is so vast, Augustus. The silence is so much larger than the forums. I feel it now—the *disinganno*. It is a *bel bell’inganno*—to think that our hands could sculpt anything other than our own disappointment.

(He shivers, pulling his thin cloak tighter as the wind picks up.)

**Augustus:**
(Standing with difficulty, he places a withered hand on Vitruvius's shoulder)
The light is gone, and the design is finished. They will call us "Augustan," they will speak of the "Golden Age," but the stone does not know its name. It only knows how to crumble.

(Augustus turns and walks toward the villa, his gait uneven and slow. Vitruvius remains on the bench, staring at the fallen column. The column lies motionless, its surface pitted by time, while a light rain begins to fall, washing away the ink of the architect’s diagrams until the paper is blank.)
