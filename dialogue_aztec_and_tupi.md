# **Dialogue between Cuitláhuac and Guairá on the Edge of the World**

**Characters:**  
- **Cuitláhuac** — An Aztec Priest, draped in heavy obsidian jewelry, his face lined with the exhaustion of maintaining the universe.  
- **Guairá** — A Tupi Warrior, unadorned and lean, carrying the quiet indifference of the forest.

**Setting:**  
*1519. A high riverbank overlooking the Atlantic. Twilight is deep purple. In the distance, the white sails of Spanish ships drift on the horizon like pale ghosts — the arrival of the “Arid Truth” (*il Vero*) that will render both men’s worlds obsolete.*

---

## **I. The Fifth Sun Falters**

**Cuitláhuac:**  
(Watching the horizon)  
Do you see them, Guairá? Those white wings on the water? They are the end of the Fifth Sun. My people have bled thousands upon the stone altars of Tenochtitlan to delay this moment. We fed the sun with the “precious water” of our veins, thinking we were the architects of the morning. Now I see that the sun would have risen anyway, indifferent to our knives.

**Guairá:**  
You have spent your life in a fever, Priest. You built mountains of stone to climb closer to gods who were never there. We Tupi followed the rivers and the seasons, moving as the wind moves. We did not owe the universe a debt of blood because we never presumed the universe was our creditor. We simply lived.

---

## **II. The Burden of Meaning**

**Cuitláhuac:**  
You lived like the jaguar — beautiful, perhaps, but hollow. My people accepted the **burden of meaning**. We turned our *infelicità* into a liturgy. If we suffered, it was for the sake of the Stars. If we died, it was to keep the Great Machine in motion. To be an Aztec was to be a pillar of the World. Now, those ships prove that the World has no pillars. It is a terrifying *nullità*.

**Guairá:**  
And there is your pride, Cuitláhuac. You would rather be a tragic slave to a machine than a free grain of sand. You call my life hollow, but your pyramids are merely a more expensive way to be miserable. Whether you chant to a stone idol or I hunt in the silence of the canopy, the *noia* — this weight in the chest — is the same. The jungle does not hear my songs, and the sky does not hear your screams.

---

## **III. The Beautiful Error**

**Cuitláhuac:**  
(Touching an obsidian pendant)  
But what becomes of the soul when the “Beautiful Error” is stripped away? If the sun moves by a law of dead mathematics and not by our sacrifice, then we are not even characters in a tragedy. We are merely insects being crushed by a foot that doesn’t even know it has stepped on us.

**Guairá:**  
We are less than insects. An insect at least struggles. I look at those ships and I feel a strange, cold peace. They are the “Truth” you feared. They come to turn your gold into stones and my forests into graves. They are the agents of a Nature that has finished with us. My “pleasant error” was thinking the forest was mine; your “noble error” was thinking the sun was yours.

---

## **IV. The Coming Void**

**Cuitláhuac:**  
I mourn the loss of the struggle. I would rather the gods were angry than realize they are absent. The void of non-existence is a small thing; it is the void of *meaninglessness* that kills me before the Spanish can reach the shore.

**Guairá:**  
(Sitting on the damp earth)  
Then sit with me, Priest. The “Arid Truth” is finally here. The sails are close enough to hear the wood creaking. Let us stop the sacrifice and the hunting.

**Cuitláhuac:**  
(Sitting beside him)  
The stars are coming out. They look the same as they did yesterday.

**Guairá:**  
That is the ultimate insult, is it not? The world is ending for us, yet the evening is perfectly still. Nature does not even have the grace to provide a storm for our departure.

**Cuitláhuac:**  
(Closing his eyes)  
It is a silent room.

**Guairá:**  
And we have finally stopped singing.
