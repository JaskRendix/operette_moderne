# **Dialogue of Hotu and Ariki on the Shore of the Final Horizon**  

**Characters:**  
- **Hotu** — A priest of the Moai, draped in a tattered feather cloak, his eyes fixed on the sky, seeking a sign of the ancestors’ return.  
- **Ariki** — A wood-carver who has seen the last of the great trees fall. He carries the quiet, heavy fatalism of one who knows the soil is spent.

**Setting:**  
*April 5th, 1722 — Easter Sunday. The coast of Rapa Nui. The air is dry and carries the scent of salt and dust. In the bay, the Dutch ship "Arend" looms like a wooden leviathan. The great stone Moai stand with their backs to the sea, their sightless eyes staring inland at a barren landscape. A single, weathered skull—the “Silent Third Presence”—lies half-buried in the red volcanic sand, unmoved by the waves.*

---

## **I. The Arrival of the Ancestors**

**Hotu:**  
(Trembling, his hand shielding his eyes from the glare)  
Do you see it, Ariki? The prophecy has birthed a miracle on the water. A floating island with white wings has come to find us in our hunger. For generations, we carved the stone and bled our hands to call them back—the *Hiva*, the ancestors. They have seen our suffering, and they have come to restore the green to the hills and the fish to the bay. Do you not feel the universe tilting back toward us?

**Ariki:**  
(Sifting the dry sand through his fingers)  
I feel only the wind, Hotu. It is the same wind that blew when the last Toromiro tree died. You see wings; I see timber—more wood than exists in the whole world, yet it does not belong to us. If the ancestors have returned, they have come in a house of stolen trees. The universe has not tilted; it has simply revealed a new horizon that does not know our names.

---

## **II. The Burden of the Stone**

**Hotu:**  
But the timing! On this day, when the sun stands so, they arrive. It must be a response to our liturgy of stone. We turned our *infelicità* into the great faces of the Moai so that the gods would never forget our faces. We sacrificed the forest to move the stone, believing that the spirit was more than the soil. Now, that ship proves that our struggle was heard! It is the axis of a new destiny.

**Ariki:**  
Harmony? You hear a drum because your heart is beating too fast. We stripped the earth to feed the stone, and now we eat the rats of the field. My face is not a revelation; it is a mask of hunger. You speak of destiny because you fear the *noia*—the vast, empty silence of a sea that leads to nowhere. But that ship is not a god. It is a thing of matter, built by men who likely have their own stones to carry.  
*[A small wave washes over the half-buried skull, dragging a few grains of sand back into the deep; the stone statues remain indifferent.]*

---

## **III. The Arid Truth**

**Ariki:**  
Listen to the truth of the waves, Hotu. It is *la natura matrigna*—the stepmother who watches her children starve and offers only salt. The men on that ship will step onto this sand, and they will see our Moai as mere rocks. They will see our hunger as a curiosity. All creatures share *l’infelicità comune*, but we were the only ones foolish enough to think our suffering made us special.

Your "Beautiful Error" was thinking the island was the center of the world. The "Arid Truth" is that we are a speck of dust in an indifferent blue throat. There is no resurrection coming from that wooden bird. There is only the *gelo*—the chill of realizing that the sea is wider than our prayers.

**Hotu:**  
(His voice breaking as he watches the first small boats lower)  
Then... the blood on the altars? The centuries of carving? The names of the kings? It was all for *il nulla*? A flicker in a dark room?

**Ariki:**  
(A pause — his voice softens, almost imperceptibly)  
Hotu… I, too, once believed the Moai were listening. When I was a child, I thought their stone eyes held the warmth of the ancestors. I carved my first statue with the hope that it would watch over us.  
(He looks at the barren hills.)  
But the world is not made of the hopes we carve into it. I mourn those illusions — they were the last trees in my heart. Yet mourning does not make them return.

---

## **IV. The Final Irony**

**Hotu:**  
(He lowers his head, the weight of the "disinganno" collapsing his chest)  
I thought I was a guardian of the world’s spirit. I thought we were the last pillars of humanity. But I see now that I have only been talking to the wind.  
(He lifts his eyes toward the vast, empty sea.)  
And for a moment… I feel the truth. A world without gods is so wide, Ariki — so wide it terrifies me. It is beautiful in its coldness, like a sky with no end.  
(A shiver runs through him.)  
It is a *bel bell’inganno*—a very beautiful error—to think the horizon ever loved us.

**Ariki:**  
(Standing up, his shadow long and thin on the red earth)  
It is an error that kept us alive while the trees died, Hotu. But the sun is high now, and the light is too bright for dreams.  
(He gestures toward the white-skinned men reaching the surf.)  
They are coming. They bring with them the names of their own gods, their own illusions. They will call this day "Easter," but for us, it is only the day the silence became too loud to ignore.

(Ariki walks toward the visitors with the heavy step of a man walking into his own grave. Hotu remains kneeling by the sightless Moai, his hand resting on the cold volcanic stone. The "Arend" shifts in the tide, its wooden hull creaking with mechanical indifference. The skull in the sand is finally uncovered by the wind, staring at the empty sky with a hollow, eternal grin.)
