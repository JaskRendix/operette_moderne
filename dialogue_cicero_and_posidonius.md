# **Dialogue of Cicero and Posidonius on the Indifferent Terrace**

**Characters:**  
- **Posidonius** — A Stoic philosopher whose mind is a map of winds, stars, and geological strata. He speaks as if the universe were listening, though it never does.  
- **Marcus Tullius Cicero** — A Roman statesman who believes that eloquence can still persuade history to behave itself.

**Setting:**  
*Rhodes, 78 BCE. A high terrace overlooking the Aegean. The sun is lowering with the same indifference it has shown to every empire before Rome and every empire after. Behind them, the library of Posidonius holds scrolls that will one day burn, though neither man sees the irony.*

---

## **I. The Illusion Makes Its Entrance**

**Cicero:**  
(With the earnestness of a man who has not yet been murdered by his own Republic)  
Master, Rome trembles. Sulla’s proscriptions have turned the Forum into a butcher’s stall. Yet I believe—no, I *know*—that if we restore harmony among the orders, if the word can once again master the sword, the Republic may yet be saved. Surely this fire in my breast is the divine spark urging me to act.

**Posidonius:**  
(Adjusting his dioptra, which is far more reliable than Roman politics)  
You feel a fever, Marcus, and call it destiny. You speak of Rome as if it were eternal marble. I have measured tides and shifting sands; marble is simply sand with delusions of grandeur. You think your oratory can halt the tide. But the tide does not speak Latin.

**Narrator:**  
It is unclear whether Cicero hears this last remark. Hope is famously hard of hearing.

---

## **II. The Dissection of Meaning, or the Lack Thereof**

**Cicero:**  
But if man is rational, must he not strive to build an earthly reflection of the heavenly order? I have devoted my life to the *Vero*—truth expressed in justice. Surely the *Logos* favors those who defend the Law.

**Posidonius:**  
The *Logos* favors nothing. It is the law of itself. You Romans mistake your temporary arrangements for cosmic architecture. Justice is not a Roman invention; it is merely the name you give to the moment before someone stronger arrives. The Republic is not dying because of wicked men, Marcus. It is dying because it has completed its cycle. The stars observe your civil wars with the same interest they grant to the molting of a goat.

**Narrator:**  
The goat, at least, does not pretend its molting is a constitutional crisis.

---

## **III. The Arid Truth, Served Cold**

**Cicero:**  
(Voice trembling, as if truth were a draft in the room)  
If the stars are silent, why do we weep? Why did Nature give us *humanitas* if we are merely atoms in a recurring conflagration? Is my love for Rome nothing but a biological error?

**Posidonius:**  
The most beautiful error of all. Nature gave you reason to perceive the machine, but pride made you believe you were its engineer. You look at the void and see a Roman profile. The universe is a vast organism breathing over eons. Your Republic is a single exhalation. Whether you save it or Caesar crushes it, the tides will rise in Gades and the stars will continue their cold geometry. You are a passenger on a ship already being dismantled for firewood, arguing with the wind about the sails.

**Narrator:**  
The wind, for its part, has never lost an argument.

---

## **Interlude: The Geometry of the Soul**

**Cicero:**  
(Stepping to the terrace edge, as if altitude could provide meaning)  
Then why do I feel compelled to stand at the prow? Why does a single sentence of a fine oration feel more real than your measurements of the moon? I have sought God in Greek philosophy, yet I find Him only in the struggle of a good man against a tyrant. Tell me—even if it is a lie—that my life’s work is not a shadow on a ruin.

**Posidonius:**  
(Softening, which is dangerous for a Stoic)  
Marcus… you ask for a significance the world does not contain. Not for Rome. Not for you. The universe is a closed circle; it has no audience. If you feel a thread between your soul and the State, it is one you spun yourself, out of fear of the *nulla*. You want to be necessary. You are merely contingent.

**Cicero:**  
Have you never felt it? When you measured the sun’s distance, did you not feel a tremor—a moment where calculation became prayer?

**Posidonius:**  
If I felt it, I recognized it as rebellion. The mind refusing its own insignificance. I cannot be your anchor, Marcus. I am drifting toward the same fire.

**Narrator:**  
It is touching, in a way, to see two brilliant men consoling each other with different brands of futility.

---

## **IV. The Final Irony**

**Cicero:**  
I will return to Rome. I will face the lions and the dictators. I will speak until my throat is dry and my head is demanded by the executioner. If the Republic is a “pleasant error,” then I will die for that error. A world of pure logic—of tides and stars and no justice—is too cold for a man to inhabit.

**Posidonius:**  
And that is the Roman tragedy. You are too noble to accept indifference. You will build monuments of words, and history will trample them. You will die for a ghost, believing it a goddess.

**Cicero:**  
Then we are both defeated. You see the truth and cannot feel it. I feel the truth and cannot see it.

**Posidonius:**  
A very Roman conclusion.  
But a conclusion nonetheless.

**Narrator:**  
One must admire Cicero’s determination to lose beautifully.

---

## **V. Cicero’s Final Monologue**

**Cicero:**  
(Alone, watching the galleys lift anchor)  
So this is the truth.  
The Law is a lantern I have carried into a collapsing tunnel, and the light reveals only the stones that will crush me.  
The stars do not care for the *Res Publica*.  
The tides do not mourn the Senate.

And yet…

What becomes of a man without his “Beautiful Error”?  
Without justice, the world is a slaughterhouse.  
The days stretch like a desert.  
The virtues lose their names.  
Even the gods grow silent.

If Rome is a dream, let me never wake.  
If it is a wound, let it be my identity.  
If it is a lie, let it be the last truth I speak before the silence.

I will keep my illusion.  
It is the only consulship I will ever truly hold.

**Narrator:**  
It is rare to see a man embrace his delusion with such dignity. Rome will reward him by cutting off his head. History, as always, applauds politely.

---

## **VI. Closing Image**

Cicero descends toward the harbor, his toga catching the salt spray.  
The sailors shout with the vigor of men who do not know they are living in a tragedy.  
Posidonius returns to his instruments, dissolving into the geometry of the evening.

A gull cries out, triumphant and meaningless.  
The sun sets with indifferent brilliance.  
Nothing has changed.  
Everything has changed.

A single ray of gold falls across a broken pillar.  
Cicero steps into it, but feels nothing.

**Narrator:**  
Thus the world rises, and Cicero carries his tomb within him.  
The universe, having made its point, resumes its silence.
